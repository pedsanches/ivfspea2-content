Reviewer #1: The manuscript presents a hybrid algorithm, IVF/SPEA2, which integrates the In Vitro Fertilization (IVF) method into the SPEA2 framework. Althought the idea of enhancing a well-established MOEA like SPEA2 is relevant, the manuscript in its current form suffers from fundamental flaws in its methodological description, experimental setup, and results analysis.
These issues are severe enough to invalidate the claimed contributions and make the work unreproducible.
1. The core of the proposed hybrid algorithm is the "IVF module." However, the manuscript fails to specify a critical piece of information: which specific IVF genetic operators were used? The text (Section 2.3) lists several operators (AR, EAR-N, EAR-T, EAR-P, EAR-PA), but it is never stated which one(s) were implemented for the extensive experiments presented. This omission makes it impossible to understand what the proposed algorithm actually does or to attempt to reproduce the results, which is a fundamental requirement for scientific publication.
2. The introduction does not build a compelling case for why SPEA2 is the ideal candidate for hybridization with IVF. The argument is primarily based on the general strengths of SPEA2 and the prior existence of IVF/NSGA-II and IVF/GDE3. A more profound analysis of SPEA2's specific shortcomings (e.g., in local search, convergence speed on certain problems) that the IVF module is uniquely positioned to address is lacking. The adaptation described in Section 4.1 feels more like a workaround for integration rather than a principled design choice driven by a clear weakness in SPEA2.
3. The results are presented primarily as a list of win/loss/tie counts and IGD values. There is a lack of in-depth analysis into why the hybrid performs better or worse on specific problems. For example, what is the mechanistic explanation from the IVF module for the improved performance on DTLZ3 or the degradation on MaF2? The connection between the algorithm's operation and the problem's characteristics (multimodality, deception, etc.) is not explored.
4. Given the methodological gaps and unverifiable figures, the strong conclusions in the abstract and highlights (e.g., "superior performance," "statistically robust convergence and diversity," "strong potential for real-world applications") are overstated and not fully supported by the evidence presented in the text.
5. The parameter values (c=10%, r=10%) are adopted from previous works without any sensitivity analysis or justification for their suitability in the context of SPEA2.
6. The choice of comparison algorithms is a significant weakness. The field of MOEAs has advanced considerably in the last decade. The selected algorithms (SPEA2, NSGA-II, NSGA-III, MOEA/D) are foundational but are now considered classic baselines. The manuscript fails to compare against any modern, state-of-the-art MOEAs from the last 3-5 years.


Reviewer #2: Here are my comments:
There is a lack of sensitivity analysis. The two core parameters of the IVF module (c=10% and r=10%) are directly adopted from previous combinations with other algorithms, without proof that these parameters are also suitable for SPEA2.
The paper mentioned that the IVF has multiple genetic operators (AR, EAR-N, etc.), but the experimental section did not specify which operator or operators were used in the 100 runs of IVF/SPEA2, nor the rationale for the selection.
In Section 4.1, the paper stated that, to adapt to SPEA2, the elite comparison criterion in the IVF module was changed from "dominance relationship" to "checking whether a new individual belongs to the top 2*c best individuals." The authors need to provide a more in-depth explanation of how this modification better aligns with the fitness logic of SPEA2.
In the experimental results, the comparison positions of IVFSPEA2 and SPEA2 are reversed. The proposed algorithm should be placed on the right, and the results should be presented in a table within the text, as the screenshot is unclear.
The abstract states, "These results confirm IVF/SPEA2 as a robust and versatile memetic algorithm for complex multi-objective optimization tasks, showing strong potential for real-world engineering and decision-making applications." However, there are no test results on real-world engineering problems to support this claim.
Please add the complexity of IVF/SPEA2 and compare it with those of the baseline algorithms.


Reviewer #3: This paper proposes a hybrid algorithm that combines the IVF method with SPEA2. The experiments are conducted on four benchmark suites to compare the performance of the proposed algorithm against several existing MOEAs. However, the methodological novelty, the rigor of the experimental evaluation, and the overall clarity of the writing require substantial improvement. The main concerns are as follows:
Comment 1：The novelty of the proposed method needs further improvement. The combination of the IVF method with SPEA2 is overly simple and results in only limited contribution to this field.
Comment 2: The description of the proposed algorithm is not sufficiently clear. The pseudocode of the main framework should be included to clearly illustrate the overall structure and workflow of the algorithm. In addition, the parameter settings of all compared algorithms should be reported to ensure the reproducibility of the experimental results.
Comment 3: The experimental results reported in Table 2 appear unconvincing. According to the comparison results, SPEA2 (proposed in 2001) significantly outperforms SPEA2+SDE and MOEA/D. It also outperforms NSGA-III on the three-objective test cases. Additional clarification or justification is needed to support these results. Considering the extensive development of MOEAs over the years, incorporating state-of-the-art algorithms as baseline methods is necessary to ensure the fairness and credibility of the performance comparison.
Comment 4: In addition to benchmark suites, some practical application problems should be included as test cases to further validate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
